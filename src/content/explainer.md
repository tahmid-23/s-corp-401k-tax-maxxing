## When this calculator matters

The U.S. retirement-savings system was built for someone with one job.
The instant you have two, a W-2 day job and an S-corp, the limits start
interacting in ways that the IRS publications don't make obvious.

### The three caps that govern you

The 402(g) employee elective deferral cap is **$24,500** in 2026, plus
$8,000 if you're 50 or older, or $11,250 if you're between 60 and 63.
This cap is _per person_, aggregated across every 401(k) you participate
in. If you defer $20k at the day job and $10k at the Solo, you've
overshot by $5.5k and you owe a corrective distribution by April 15.

The 415(c) annual additions limit is **$72,000** per _unrelated_
employer. Your day job and your S-corp each get their own $72k bucket.
Catch-up contributions sit outside this limit.

The Solo 401(k) employer side is capped at **25% of your S-corp W-2
wages**. This is the only way to push the Solo plan above the
elective-deferral limit, and it's the reason paying yourself more
S-corp salary is sometimes worth it.

### The hidden cost of S-corp W-2

Once your day-job W-2 crosses the Social Security wage base ($184,500
in 2026), every additional dollar of S-corp salary triggers a 6.2%
employer-side SS payment that your S-corp **cannot get back**. The
employee side comes back to you on Form 1040 Schedule 3 as excess SS
withholding. The employer payment does not.

On top of that, every dollar of W-2 pays 2.9% Medicare (both halves
combined), and another 0.9% additional Medicare above your
filing-status threshold. The total drag on the marginal S-corp wage
dollar, in the scenario where your day-job W-2 has already maxed Social
Security, is roughly 10%. Against that 10% cost, each dollar of S-corp
W-2 unlocks 25 cents of additional Solo 401(k) employer contribution.
The arithmetic only favors more salary up to a point.

### QBI cuts the other way

The 20% pass-through deduction under Section 199A applies to your
S-corp's _distributions_, not your W-2 wages. Below the income
threshold ($201,775 single, $403,500 joint in 2026), paying yourself
less salary leaves more profit eligible for the 20% deduction.

Above the threshold the math flips. For non-SSTBs the W-2 wage limit
starts binding, which means paying yourself _more_ W-2 can grow the
deduction. For SSTBs (consulting, law, accounting, health, financial
services and similar) the deduction phases out completely.

### Reasonable compensation

The IRS expects you to pay yourself a "reasonable" S-corp wage for the
work you actually perform. Setting W-2 to zero or to an obviously low
number to dodge FICA invites reclassification, and the case law is not
on your side. _Watson v. United States_ and _David E. Watson PC v.
United States_ are the ones cited most often.

The calculator does not enforce a floor. It does flag a warning when
your S-corp W-2 looks low relative to net profit, but the right number
depends on what you actually do for the business and what comparable
positions pay.
